## Quick Summary

- Governance is the conversion of constitutional values and public policy into real outcomes.
- GS II asks how policies are designed, implemented, monitored, corrected, and made accountable.
- Important recurring themes: transparency, accountability, e-governance, citizens charters, grievance redressal, social audit, civil services, NGOs, SHGs, and stakeholder participation.

## Source Anchors

- DARPG records Citizen Charters as service-delivery commitments with service standards, time limits, and grievance-redress avenues.
- DARPG's public grievance work includes CPGRAMS as a central grievance redressal and monitoring system.
- UPSC syllabus explicitly lists transparency, accountability, e-governance, citizen charters, and institutional measures.

## Prelims Pointers

- Governance is more Mains-heavy, but Prelims may ask institutions, portals, acts, and reports.
- Know the difference between transparency tools, accountability bodies, grievance mechanisms, and service delivery platforms.

## Mains Pointers

- Governance answers should include design and implementation.
- Use citizen perspective: access, affordability, awareness, grievance, inclusion.
- Mention risks: digital exclusion, privacy, capacity gaps, corruption, centralization, weak evaluation.
- Use reforms: social audit, open data, capacity building, decentralization, outcome budgeting, independent evaluation.

## Revision Summary

- Governance is about outcomes, not only institutions.
- Policy failure often happens at design, capacity, coordination, or feedback stages.
- Citizen Charters need measurable standards and grievance redress.
- E-governance must be inclusive and privacy-aware.
- Accountability requires information, answerability, and consequences.
