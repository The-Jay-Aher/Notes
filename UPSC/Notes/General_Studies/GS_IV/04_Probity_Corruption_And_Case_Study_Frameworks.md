## Quick Summary

- Probity means honesty, transparency, accountability, and ethical use of public authority.
- Corruption is not only bribery; it includes conflict of interest, favouritism, discretion abuse, leakage, collusion, and misuse of public office.
- Case studies require structured judgement, not emotional reaction.

## High-Yield Exam Notes

## Topic: Probity In Governance

## Meaning:
- Probity is uprightness in public life: honesty, integrity, transparency, accountability, fairness, and responsible use of power and public funds.

## Why important:
- Builds trust.
- Prevents corruption.
- Protects public funds.
- Improves service quality.
- Strengthens legitimacy.
- Reduces arbitrariness.

## Components:
- Public service orientation.
- Transparency.
- Accountability.
- Integrity.
- Financial propriety.
- Conflict-of-interest control.
- Rule of law.
- Citizen-centric service.

## Topic: Philosophical Basis Of Governance And Probity

## Constitutional basis:
- Justice, liberty, equality, fraternity.
- Rule of law.
- Fundamental rights.
- Directive principles.
- Public trust doctrine.

## Ethical basis:
- Public office is a trust.
- Citizens are ends, not instruments.
- Power must be justified by public purpose.
- Procedure protects fairness.

## Topic: Information Sharing And Transparency

## Meaning:
- Making relevant public information accessible, understandable, timely, and usable.

## Tools:
- RTI.
- Proactive disclosure.
- Open data.
- Social audit.
- Public dashboards.
- Reasoned orders.
- Citizen charters.

## Benefits:
- Reduces corruption.
- Empowers citizens.
- Improves accountability.
- Helps media and civil society oversight.
- Builds trust.

## Limits:
- National security.
- Privacy.
- Confidential deliberation where legally protected.
- Ongoing investigation.

## Balance:
- Use exemptions narrowly and with reasons; secrecy should serve lawful public interest, not convenience.

## Topic: Right To Information

## Importance:
- Gives citizens a legal route to seek information from public authorities.
- Supports transparency, accountability, participation, and anti-corruption.

## Governance impact:
- Forces record-keeping.
- Enables public scrutiny.
- Helps expose leakage and delay.
- Strengthens citizen voice.

## Concerns:
- Delays.
- Vexatious requests.
- Threats to information seekers.
- Poor proactive disclosure.
- Capacity constraints.

## Topic: Codes Of Ethics And Codes Of Conduct

## Code of ethics:
- Broad principles and values that should guide conduct.

## Code of conduct:
- Specific rules on acceptable and prohibited behaviour.

## Difference:
- Ethics code answers "what values should guide me?"
- Conduct code answers "what must I do or avoid?"

## Need:
- Clarity.
- Training.
- Enforcement.
- Leadership example.
- Protection for honest officials.

## Topic: Citizen Charters

## Meaning:
- A written declaration by a service provider on service standards, choice, accessibility, non-discrimination, transparency, accountability, and grievance redressal.

## Benefits:
- Clarifies service standards.
- Empowers citizens.
- Changes public servant orientation from power to duty.
- Supports transparency and accountability.

## Problems:
- Vague standards.
- No compensation for delay.
- Poor awareness.
- Weak monitoring.
- Lack of citizen participation in drafting.

## Improvement:
- Specific timelines.
- Measurable standards.
- Public display.
- Online tracking.
- Independent evaluation.
- Grievance link.
- Periodic update.

## Topic: Work Culture

## Meaning:
- Shared norms, habits, incentives, leadership style, and daily practices in an organization.

## Good work culture:
- Integrity.
- Timeliness.
- Teamwork.
- Respect.
- Learning.
- Citizen orientation.
- Documentation.
- Feedback.

## Bad work culture:
- Delay.
- Hierarchy without responsibility.
- Blame shifting.
- Informality in decisions.
- Fear.
- Corruption tolerance.

## Reform:
- Leadership example.
- Clear goals.
- Process simplification.
- Training.
- Performance review.
- Technology with accountability.
- Staff welfare.

## Topic: Quality Of Service Delivery

## Dimensions:
- Access.
- Affordability.
- Timeliness.
- Reliability.
- Dignity.
- Non-discrimination.
- Grievance redressal.
- Outcome quality.

## Topic: Utilization Of Public Funds

## Ethical issues:
- Wasteful expenditure.
- Diversion.
- Leakage.
- Last-minute spending.
- Poor procurement.
- Inflated contracts.
- Weak monitoring.

## Safeguards:
- Outcome budgeting.
- E-procurement.
- Audit.
- Social audit.
- Public dashboards.
- Third-party evaluation.
- Strong internal controls.
- Geo-tagging where useful.

## Topic: Corruption

## Meaning:
- Abuse of public office or entrusted power for private gain.

## Forms:
- Bribery.
- Nepotism.
- Favouritism.
- Cronyism.
- Embezzlement.
- Extortion.
- Collusive procurement.
- Regulatory capture.
- Conflict of interest.
- Revolving door misuse.

## Causes:
- Discretion without transparency.
- Complex procedures.
- Low detection risk.
- Political-business nexus.
- Weak accountability.
- Social tolerance.
- Delay and scarcity.

## Consequences:
- Loss of public trust.
- Poor service delivery.
- Fiscal loss.
- Inequality.
- Criminalization.
- Lower investment quality.
- Administrative cynicism.

## Anti-corruption strategy:
- Simplify processes.
- Reduce unnecessary discretion.
- Digitize with audit trail.
- Protect whistleblowers.
- Independent investigation.
- Timely prosecution.
- Public disclosure.
- Citizen feedback.
- Ethical leadership.

## Case Study Framework

## Step 1: Facts
- List verified facts.
- Mention missing information.
- Avoid assumptions.

## Step 2: Stakeholders
- Citizens.
- Vulnerable groups.
- Public officials.
- Elected representatives.
- Contractors/private parties.
- Environment.
- Future generations.
- Institution.

## Step 3: Values
- Law.
- Integrity.
- Impartiality.
- Compassion.
- Transparency.
- Accountability.
- Efficiency.
- Public interest.

## Step 4: Conflicts
- Law versus compassion.
- Speed versus fairness.
- Transparency versus confidentiality.
- Personal loyalty versus public duty.
- Development versus environment.
- Security versus liberty.

## Step 5: Options
- Minimum three feasible options.
- Include status quo only if realistic.
- Avoid illegal or theatrical options unless used to reject them.

## Step 6: Evaluation
- Legal effect.
- Ethical effect.
- Administrative feasibility.
- Impact on vulnerable groups.
- Short-term and long-term consequences.
- Institutional precedent.

## Step 7: Decision
- Choose lawful, fair, practical, and compassionate action.
- Explain why rejected options are weaker.

## Step 8: Safeguards
- Written record.
- Consultation.
- Public communication where appropriate.
- Grievance redressal.
- Audit.
- Follow-up review.

## Prelims Pointers

- Probity terms overlap with governance but are mainly Mains-oriented.
- Know definitions: probity, corruption, transparency, accountability, citizen charter, conflict of interest.

## Mains Pointers

- In probity answers, combine values with mechanisms.
- In corruption answers, include prevention, detection, punishment, and culture change.
- In case studies, never ignore legality, vulnerable groups, documentation, and accountability.

## Keywords for Answer Writing

- Public office as trust
- Transparency
- Accountability
- RTI
- Citizen charter
- Conflict of interest
- Public funds
- Social audit
- Whistleblower
- Rule of law

## Possible UPSC Questions

- Probity in governance requires both personal integrity and institutional design. Discuss.
- Explain the role of RTI in strengthening transparency and accountability.
- Distinguish between code of ethics and code of conduct.
- How can citizen charters improve service delivery?
- A contractor offers you a favour before tender evaluation. What will you do?

## Things To Revise Repeatedly

- Probity definition.
- RTI, citizen charter, social audit.
- Corruption forms and causes.
- Case-study framework.
- Stakeholder and value lists.

## Revision Summary

- Probity is ethical governance in action.
- Transparency, RTI, citizen charters, audit, and grievance redressal are practical probity tools.
- Case studies require structured, lawful, fair, and compassionate decisions.
